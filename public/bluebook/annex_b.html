Annex B

Standard Debate Formats and Procedures

Standard Formats, Adjudication, and Tournament Organization

Section 1. Purpose and Scope.

This Annex establishes the standard debate formats, procedures, and adjudication criteria for all Society-sanctioned debates, including inter-House tournaments, the Inter-House Debate Cup, practice debates, and external competitions where the Society adopts its own format. These procedures shall govern the conduct of debates to ensure fairness, intellectual rigor, and consistency across all competitive activities. This Annex operates in conjunction with Article I (House Point System) and Article III (Debate League) of the Rules and Procedures.

Section 2. Authorized Debate Formats.

The Society recognizes the following debate formats for official competitions. The High Council shall determine the applicable format for each tournament prior to the commencement of the competition.

(1) Oxford-Oregon Format

A three-on-three parliamentary-style debate format.

(a) Two Teams. The Affirmative (Government) and Negative (Opposition), each composed of three speakers.

(b) Speech Structure. Three constructive speeches per team, followed by a rebuttal speech from each team.

(c) Time Limits. Constructive speeches shall not exceed seven (7) minutes. Rebuttal speeches shall not exceed five (5) minutes. The Timekeeper shall provide warnings at the following intervals:

Time MarkerSignal
2 minutes remainingAudible bell or verbal "Two minutes"
1 minute remainingAudible bell or verbal "One minute"
30 seconds remainingAudible bell or verbal "Thirty seconds"
Time expiredAudible bell or verbal "Time"

(d) Points of Information (POI). POIs are permitted during constructive speeches only. POIs shall not exceed fifteen (15) seconds. The speaker may accept or decline a POI at their discretion. There is no universal minimum or maximum number of POIs in standard Oxford-Oregon. However, the Society may adopt specific POI requirements for its internal tournaments by resolution of the High Council. If such requirements are adopted, they shall be published in the tournament guidelines at least seven (7) days prior to the competition.

(e) Speaker Order:

OrderSpeakerRole
1First Affirmative(Constructive Speech) Introduce and define the motion; present core arguments
2First Negative(Constructive Speech) Rebut Affirmative arguments; present negative case
3Second Affirmative(Constructive Speech) Defend Affirmative case; advance additional arguments
4Second Negative(Constructive Speech) Rebut Affirmative case; advance negative arguments
5Third Affirmative(Constructive Speech) Rebut Negative case; consolidate Affirmative position
6Third Negative(Constructive Speech) Rebut Affirmative case; consolidate Negative position
7Affirmative Rebuttal(Rebuttal Speech) Summarize and defend Affirmative case (5 min)
8Negative Rebuttal(Rebuttal Speech) Summarize and defend Negative case (5 min)

(f) Preparation Time. Each team shall be granted a total of fifteen (15) minutes of preparation time after the motion is announced and before the debate begins. No preparation time shall be granted between speeches. The fifteen (15) minutes shall be strictly timed and announced by the Timekeeper, who shall signal when five (5) minutes remain and when time expires.

(2) British Parliamentary Format (BP)

A four-team format commonly used in internal competitions.

(a) Teams. The debate consists of four teams, each composed of two speakers:

Government: Opening Government + Closing Government

Opposition: Opening Opposition + Closing Opposition

(b) Speech Structure. Each of the four teams consists of two speakers. The debate consists of eight speeches delivered in the following order:

OrderSpeakerTitle
1Opening GovernmentPrime Minister
2Opening OppositionLeader of Opposition
3Opening GovernmentDeputy Prime Minister
4Opening OppositionDeputy Leader of Opposition
5Closing GovernmentMember of Government
6Closing OppositionMember of Opposition
7Closing GovernmentGovernment Whip
8Closing OppositionOpposition Whip

(c) Time Limits. Each speech shall not exceed seven (7) minutes.

(d) Timekeeping Signals. Standard BP timekeeping shall be as follows:

Time MarkerSignal
1 minute elapsedSingle knock
6 minutes elapsedSingle knock (with optional visual cue, e.g., raised hand or yellow card)
7 minutes elapsedDouble knock (time expired)

No countdown warnings are given in standard BP. All speakers must conclude within the seven (7) minute limit. If a speaker continues past the double knock, the Timekeeper shall immediately signal for them to stop.

(e) Points of Information (POI). POIs are permitted during all speeches except the Whip speeches (Government Whip and Opposition Whip). This includes:

AllowedNot Allowed
Prime MinisterGovernment Whip
Leader of OppositionOpposition Whip
Deputy Prime Minister—
Deputy Leader of Opposition—
Member of Government—
Member of Opposition—

POIs shall not exceed fifteen (15) seconds. The speaker may accept or decline a POI at their discretion.

(f) Opening and Closing Team Roles.

(i) Opening Government (OG): Introduces the Government's first substantive arguments. Defines the motion and establishes the framework for the debate.

(ii) Opening Opposition (OO): Introduces the Opposition's first substantive arguments. Challenges the Government's definition and presents the Opposition's case.

(iii) Closing Government (CG): Introduces new arguments that distinguish them from Opening Government. Must remain consistent with the Government's overall position but must add substantive value beyond what OG presented.

(iv) Closing Opposition (CO): Introduces new arguments that distinguish them from Opening Opposition. Must remain consistent with the Opposition's overall position but must add substantive value beyond what OO presented.

(v) Whip Speeches (Government Whip and Opposition Whip): Serve as the final speeches for each side. They must:

(A) Summarize the debate from their side's perspective;

(B) Compare their team's arguments to the opposing side;

(C) Identify and explain why their side has won the debate;

(D) Not introduce new arguments (new analysis or new examples may be permitted, but not entirely new case material).

(g) Preparation Time. Each team shall be granted (15) minutes of preparation time after the motion is announced and before the debate begins. No preparation time shall be granted between speeches.

(3) Asian Parliamentary Format

A three-on-three format used in Asian debating circuits.

(a) Two Teams. Government and Opposition, each composed of three speakers.

(b) Speech Structure. The debate consists of six speeches delivered in the following order:

OrderSpeaker
1Prime Minister
2Leader of Opposition
3Deputy Prime Minister
4Deputy Leader of Opposition
5Government Whip
6Opposition Whip

(c) Time Limits. Each speech shall not exceed seven (7) minutes.

(d) Timekeeping Signals. The Timekeeper shall provide warnings at the following intervals:

Time MarkerSignal
2 minutes remainingAudible bell or verbal "Two minutes"
1 minute remainingAudible bell or verbal "One minute"
30 seconds remainingAudible bell or verbal "Thirty seconds"
Time expiredAudible bell or verbal "Time"

(e) Points of Information. POIs are permitted during all speeches except Whip speeches. POIs shall not exceed fifteen (15) seconds.

(f) Whip Speeches. Whip speeches must summarize the debate, compare arguments, and explain why their side has won. No new arguments shall be introduced.

(g) Preparation Time. Each team shall be granted twenty (20) minutes of preparation time after the motion is announced and before the debate begins. No preparation time shall be granted between speeches.

(4) Other Formats.

The High Council may authorize additional formats for specific tournaments, provided that:

(a) The format is announced at least fourteen (14) days prior to the competition;

(b) The format's rules and procedures are published in writing to all participating teams;

(c) The format aligns with the Society's principles of intellectual rigor and fair competition.

(9) Breaks and Wellness.

(a) Mandatory Wellness Breaks. In accordance with Article 3, Section 13 of the Constitution and Article IV, Section 4(5)(f) of these Rules, tournaments shall include mandatory wellness breaks to support member well-being and sustained engagement.

(b) Break Scheduling. The Tournament Director shall schedule breaks at intervals that prevent excessive fatigue and maintain participant well-being. The timing and frequency of breaks shall be determined based on:

(i) The total duration of the tournament;

(ii) The number of debate rounds;

(iii) Meal times;

(iv) Venue constraints;

(v) Participant feedback.

(c) Break Announcement. The Tournament Director shall announce all breaks at the start of the tournament and provide reminders prior to each break.

(d) Break Activities. Breaks may be used for rest, hydration, meals, and brief personal respite. Participants are encouraged to step away from debate preparation during breaks.

(e) Exceptions. In cases of emergency or extraordinary circumstances, the Tournament Director may adjust break schedules. Such adjustments shall be communicated to all participants.

Section 4. Motion Setting and Release.

(1) Motion Categories. Debate motions shall be classified into the following categories:

(a) Policy Motions. Motions proposing a specific policy change, framed as "This House would [policy action]" or "This House supports [policy action]."

(b) Value Motions. Motions debating moral or philosophical propositions, framed as "This House believes that [value proposition]" or "This House regrets [state of affairs]."

(c) Fact Motions. Motions debating empirical claims, framed as "This House believes that [factual claim]."

(2) Motion Selection.

(a) For Inter-House Debate Cup matches and Society-organized open tournaments, motions shall be prepared by a Motion Committee composed of:

(b) The Motion Committee shall prepare a bank of at least ten (10) motions per category, ensuring diversity of topics and alignment with the Society's educational mission.

Section 5. Adjudication Criteria.

(1) Adjudication Panels.

(a) Each debate match shall be adjudicated by a panel of at least three (3) qualified judges.

(b) Judges shall be appointed by the Tournament Director in consultation with the Secretary of Internal Affairs.

(c) Judges must be active members in good standing who have completed at least one (1) academic semester of active participation in the Society's debate activities, training, or mentorship programs, or alumni members who have demonstrated proficiency in the applicable debate format.

(d) No judge shall adjudicate a match involving their own House or a team they have coached or mentored, to avoid conflicts of interests.

(e) The Chief Adjudicator shall oversee all judges, resolve disputes, and ensure consistency in adjudication.

(2) Adjudication Criteria for Oxford-Oregon Format.

Judges shall evaluate teams based on the following weighted criteria:

CriterionWeightDescription
Content40%Quality of arguments, evidence, and analysis
Style30%Clarity, rhetoric, and delivery
Strategy30%Responsiveness, refutation, and time management

(3) Adjudication Criteria for British Parliamentary Format.

In standard BP tournaments, judges do not use fixed percentage weights. Instead, judges rank teams 1st to 4th based on their overall contribution to the debate. The ranking is holistic and considers:

(a) Quality of arguments and analysis (relevance, depth, reasoning, use of evidence);

(b) Responsiveness (engagement with opposing arguments, ability to adapt to the debate);

(c) Clarity and persuasion (delivery, organization, rhetorical effectiveness);

(d) Strategic positioning (team coordination, meeting burdens, distinguishing from Opening/Closing counterparts, effective use of POIs).

Verdict Determination. Each judge shall rank the four teams from 1st (best) to 4th (worst). The team with the highest total ranking (with 1st receiving 4 points, 2nd receiving 3 points, 3rd receiving 2 points, and 4th receiving 1 point) shall be declared the winner. In the event of a tie in total ranking points, the Chair of the adjudication panel shall have the casting vote to determine the final ranking.

(4) Adjudication Criteria for Asian Parliamentary Format.

Judges shall evaluate teams based on the following weighted criteria:

CriterionWeightDescription
Content40%Quality of arguments, evidence, and analysis
Style30%Clarity, rhetoric, and delivery
Strategy30%Responsiveness, refutation, and time management

(5) Individual Speaker Awards.

(a) The Best Speaker award shall be given to the individual who demonstrates the highest overall debating skill, judged on:

(i) Clarity and persuasiveness of arguments (30%)

(ii) Effective use of evidence and reasoning (25%)

(iii) Oratory and delivery (25%)

(iv) Responsiveness to opposing arguments (20%)

(b) Individual speaker scores shall be submitted by each judge after every debate round. The speaker with the highest cumulative score across all rounds shall be declared the Best Speaker of the tournament.

(c) In the event of a tie, the following tiebreakers shall apply in order:

(i) The speaker whose team won the most debate rounds;

(ii) The speaker with the highest score in the final round;

(iii) The speaker who demonstrated the most effective engagement with POIs (based on judge feedback);

(iv) Drawing of lots.

(6) Adjudication Procedure.

(a) Judges shall submit written ballots within fifteen (15) minutes of the conclusion of each debate.

(b) Ballots shall include:

(i) The winning team for each round;

(ii) Individual speaker scores (on a scale of 0-100, with 70 representing an average performance);

(iii) A brief justification for the decision, identifying key factors;

(iv) The name of the Best Speaker for that round.

(c) If the panel is divided, the majority decision shall prevail. In the event of a 1-1-1 split (three judges with different winners), the Tournament Director shall break the tie based on average speaker scores.

(d) Teams shall have the right to request written feedback from the adjudication panel. Feedback shall be provided in writing within three (3) business days of the competition's conclusions.

Section 7. Procedure for Points of Information (POI).

(1) General Principles.

(a) POIs are intended to promote active engagement and intellectual rigor, not to disrupt or intimidate.

(b) POIs shall be offered respectfully and concisely.

(c) The speaker has the right to accept or decline POIs at their discretion. Declining a POI is acceptable and is a legitimate strategic choice, particularly if accepting it would disrupt the flow of the speech.

(d) The key consideration in evaluating POI engagement is whether the speaker is substantively engaging with the opposing side's arguments, not the number of POIs accepted.

(2) Criteria for Acceptable POIs.

A POI must:

(a) Be a question or brief challenge to the speaker's argument;

(b) Not exceed fifteen (15) seconds in duration;

(c) Be delivered in a civil and professional tone;

(d) Not be a personal attack or ad hominem argument; and

(e) Not introduce new arguments that should have been presented in the speaker's own speech.

(3) POI Procedure by Format.

(a) Oxford-Oregon Format. POIs are permitted during constructive speeches only. POIs are not permitted during rebuttal speeches.

(b) British Parliamentary Format. POIs are permitted during all speeches except the Whip speeches (Government Whip and Opposition Whip).

(c) Asian Parliamentary Format. POIs are permitted during all speeches except Whip speeches.

(4) POI Conduct.

(a) To offer a POI, a member of the opposing team shall stand and say "Point of Information" or "On that point." The member shall remain standing until acknowledged or declined.

(b) The speaker may acknowledge the POI by saying "Yes" or "Accepted," or decline by saying "No" or "Not at this time."

(c) If accepted, the questioning member shall deliver the POI within fifteen (15) seconds.

(d) The speaker shall respond to the POI within the remainder of their speaking time.

(e) Accepting and responding effectively to POIs shall be considered positively in the Method/Strategy criterion (Oxford-Oregon) or overall contribution (BP).

(f) Declining POIs is acceptable if done respectfully. A pattern of declining all POIs without substantive engagement with opposing arguments may be considered negatively in the Method/Strategy criterion, but declining POIs to maintain flow is a legitimate strategic choice that shall not be penalized in itself.

(g) POIs that are disruptive, disrespectful, or exceeding time limits shall result in a -2 point deduction from the offering member's individual speaker score.

(5) POI Log. The Timekeeper shall maintain a POI Log recording:

(a) Each POI offered, accepted, or declined;

(b) The time during the speech when offered;

(c) Any violations of POI conduct rules.

(d) The POI Log shall be submitted to the Tournament Director with the Time Log.

Section 8. Code of Conduct for Debaters.

(1) General Standards.

(a) Debaters shall treat all participants – opponents, judges, and audience members – with respect and civility.

(b) Debaters shall not engage in personal attacks, harassment, or discriminatory language.

(c) Debaters shall adhere to the rules of the debate format and the rulings of the Presiding Officer and Timekeeper.

(d) Debaters shall not use electronic devices during speeches, except as necessary for reading prepared materials or accessing evidence as permitted by the format.

(2) Academic Integrity.

(a) Debaters shall properly cite sources and evidence used in their arguments.

(b) Fabrication of evidence, misrepresentation of sources, or fraudulent data shall constitute a Major Violation under Article VI.

(c) Debaters shall disclose the use of AI tools in the preparation of their cases, as required by the AI-Assisted Case & Research Disclosure Template established in these Rules.

(3) Disruptive Behavior.

(a) Any behavior that materially disrupts a debate match shall be grounds for:

BehaviorSanction
First minor disruption (talking over, interrupting)Warning from Presiding Officer
Second minor disruption-5 points from speaker's individual score
Major disruption (shouting, harassment, leaving seat)Immediate removal from debate; team may forfeit

(b) The Presiding Officer shall have the authority to suspend a debate and rule on disruptive conduct. The decision of the Presiding Officer may be appealed to the Tournament Director, whose decision shall be final.

(4) Dress Code.

No mandatory dress code shall be imposed for any tournament, provided that the attire worn does not contravene any provision of the BSU Student Code of Conduct. Notwithstanding the foregoing, members are encouraged to wear attire that appropriately identifies or represents their respective House or the Society during participation in internal or external competitions.

(5) Representation of the Society.

(a) Members representing the Society in external competitions shall conduct themselves in a manner that upholds the Society's reputation.

(b) Any conduct that brings disrepute to the Society shall constitute a Major Violation under Article VI, and may result in:

(i) Disqualification from the Debate League;

(ii) Revocation of Society support for future competition; and

(iii) Referral to the High Tribunal for disciplinary proceedings.

Section 9. Dispute Resolution.

(1) Types of Disputes.

TypeDescriptionExamples
Minor DisputesBrief, low-impact issues resolved on the spotDisputed time signals, brief interruptions, minor procedural questions
Major DisputesSerious issues requiring formal adjudicationAllegations of bias, severe rule violations, eligibility disputes

(2) Minor Dispute Resolution.

(a) Teams may raise minor disputes with the Tournament Director, who shall resolve them on the spot.

(b) The Tournament Director's decision on minor disputes shall be final and not subject to appeal.

(3) Major Dispute Resolution.

(a) Teams may file a formal protest for major disputes in writing to the Tournament Director:

(i) The protest must specify the grounds and provide supporting evidence;

(ii) The Tournament Director shall convene an emergency adjudication panel to review the protest;

(iii) The panel shall render a decision within one (1) hour of the protest filing.

(b) The emergency adjudication panel shall be composed of:

MemberRole
Tournament DirectorChair (non-voting)
Chief AdjudicatorVoting member
One judge not involved in the disputed debateVoting member
Secretary of Internal Affairs or designeeVoting member

(c) The panel's decision shall be by majority vote. In the event of a tie, the Tournament Director shall cast the deciding vote.

(4) Appeals to the High Council President.

(a) The decision of the emergency adjudication panel may be appealed to the High Council President within twenty-four (24) hours of the decision.

(b) The appeal must be submitted in writing and specify the grounds for appeal.

(c) The President shall render a decision within forty-eight (48) hours of receiving the appeal.

(d) The President's decision shall be final and executory.

(5) Dispute Resolution Log. The Tournament Director shall maintain a Dispute Resolution Log recording:

(a) All disputes raised, including date, time, and parties involved;

(b) The nature of each dispute and how it was resolved;

(c) Any sanctions or penalties imposed;

(d) The log shall be submitted to the Secretary of Internal Affairs with the post-tournament report.

Section 10. External Competition Standards.

(1) Representation Standards.

(a) Members selected to represent the Society in external competitions under Article III (Debate League) shall:

(i) Demonstrate proficiency in the applicable debate format;

(ii) Maintain an active participation rate in Society activities;

(iii) Adhere to the Society's code of conduct at all times;

(iv) Submit required documentation (e.g., pre-tournament preparation plans, post-tournament reports).

(b) Selection criteria for external competitions shall include:

(i) Individual Debate Point ranking;

(ii) Performance in Society-sanctioned tournaments;

(iii) Availability and commitment; and

(iv) Demonstrated ability to work effectively in a team.

(2) Preparation for External Competitions.

(a) Debate League members selected for external competitions shall engage in preparatory activities as coordinated by the Secretary of Internal Affairs:

(i) Pre-tournament workshops (minimum 2 sessions);

(ii) Practice debates (minimum 3 matches);

(iii) Case-building sessions (minimum 3 sessions); and

(iv) Research and preparation supervision.

(b) The Society shall provide access to:

(i) The Society's case library and research resources;

(ii) Mentorship from senior members or alumni;

(iii) Practice debate facilities; and

(iv) Logistical support for travel and accommodation, subject to Article III, Section 4.

(3) Post-Tournament Reporting.

(a) Within seven (7) calendar days of the conclusion of an external tournament, the member(s) shall submit a Post-Tournament Report to the Secretary of Internal Affairs.

(b) The Report shall include:

(i) Tournament name, date, and location;

(ii) Team members and roles;

(iii) Summary of debate rounds and outcomes;

(iv) Key lessons learned and areas for improvement;

(v) Recommendations for future participants;

(vi) Acknowledgement of Society support provided.

(c) The Secretary of Internal Affairs shall compile the reports and submit a summary to the High Council for review and archiving.

(4) External Competition Fees and Resources.

(a) Competition fees, registration costs, and travel expenses for Debate League members selected for external competitions shall be managed in accordance with Article V (Financial and Resource Management Protocols).

(b) Reimbursement requests shall be submitted with the Post-Tournament Report.

(c) The High Council shall prioritize funding for tournaments that:

(i) Have the highest prestige and relevance to the Society's mission;

(ii) Offer the greatest skill development opportunities for members; and

(iii) Provide the best return on investment in terms of exposure and network building.

Section 11. Practice Debates.

(1) Purpose and Eligibility.

(a) Practice debates are intended to help members develop debating skills, test arguments, and prepare for competitions.

(b) Practice debates may be organized by:

(i) A House Council for its members;

(ii) A team preparing for an external competition;

(iii) The High Council for Society-wide skill development.

(c) Any active member may participate in practice debates.

(2) Approval and Point Eligibility.

(a) Practice debates must be approved by the High Council to be eligible for Individual Debate Points under Article III.

(b) Approval shall be granted if:

(i) The practice debate has defined format and rules;

(ii) Qualified adjudicators are appointed;

(iii) A Timekeeper is designated;

(iv) Results are documented and submitted to the Secretary of Internal Affairs.

(3) Practice Debate Standards.

(a) Practice debates should follow the procedures outlined in this Annex.

(b) Feedback shall be provided to participants by adjudicators.

(c) A Practice Debate Log shall be maintained by the organizer, recording:

(i) Date and time of practice debate;

(ii) Participants and teams;

(iii) Format used and motion debated;

(iv) Adjudication results;

(v) Feedback summary.

(4) Practice Debate Best Practices.

(a) Organizers are encouraged to:

(i) Rotate roles so members gain experience in all speaker positions;

(ii) Use a variety of debate formats and motion types;

(iii) Incorporate training exercises (e.g., POI practice, rebuttal drills) into sessions;

(iv) Record sessions for self-review (with participants' consent).

(b) Experienced members and Debate League members are encouraged to mentor novice participants in practice debates.
